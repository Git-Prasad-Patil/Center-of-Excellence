# 📝 My Notes: Advice for Giving Feedback

**Source:** [Advice for Giving Feedback](https://www.industriallogic.com/blog/advice-for-giving-feedback/) — Tara Scott & Tim Ottinger, Industrial Logic (Mar 22, 2021) **Read time:** ~8 min **Companion piece to check out:** [Rules for Receiving Feedback](https://www.industriallogic.com/blog/rules-for-receiving-feedback)

---

## 💡 Core Idea

The whole article boils down to one guiding intention:

> 🎯 **"Make People Awesome."**

Feedback isn't about venting or "winning" — it's a gift meant to help someone grow. Good feedback builds trust 🤝 and speeds up team performance; bad feedback breeds defensiveness 🛡️ and kills productivity.

---

## 🎬 The Story That Sets It Up

A developer got frustrated with an architect during a planning meeting. The moment the architect stepped out, the developer started venting sarcastically about him to the room.

**Takeaway:** His _concerns_ may have been valid, but venting behind someone's back is the wrong _approach_. This is basically the whole article in miniature — separate the **functional** problem (the design issue) from the **behavioral** problem (how it was handled).

---

## 🧭 Before You Even Open Your Mouth (Steps 1–5)

These are all about _you_, not the other person. Get your own house in order first.

1. **🌡️ Check your emotional state.** Angry, exasperated, resentful? You'll speak _from_ yourself, not _to_ them. Watch out for the [Fundamental Attribution Error](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AdrDAik86rc) — we love to assume bad intentions in others. Stick to [behavior, not imagined motives](https://www.radicalcandor.com/dont-personalize-feedback/).
    
2. **🎯 Check your motivation.** Are you doing this to feel better yourself, or to genuinely help the relationship/team? Do you actually [care personally](https://www.radicalcandor.com/faq/what-does-it-mean-to-care-personally/) about their success?
    
3. **✅ Make sure you have permission.** Feedback lands better when it's invited — via agreement, company norms, a direct request, or them asking you.
    
4. **🤝 Consider your relationship.** How well do you know them? What tone/approach will actually land?
    
5. **🔒 Talk privately.** Never publicly. One-on-one, always.
    

> 🗒️ _Personal note: #1 and #2 feel like the real filter here — if I'm venting to feel better, I should probably wait._

---

## 🛠️ Steps 6–9: Delivering It

6. **📋 Have a plan — use the SBIN framework:**
    
    |Letter|Meaning|
    |---|---|
    |**S**ituation|What/where/when|
    |**B**ehavior|What you _observed_ — no opinions. ("I heard you say 'X'" > "you seemed frustrated")|
    |**I**mpact|Who was affected, and how?|
    |**N**ext|What do we do differently going forward?|
    
7. **⏱️ Timing matters.** Give feedback promptly — but not so fast you're still reactive. Timebox your prep.
    
8. **⚖️ Don't make it only correction.** Balance criticism with genuine appreciation, or you risk the "what the hell" effect — where someone gives up trying because they feel they'll never be good enough.
    
9. **🔄 Make it bi-directional.** Invite feedback back. Ask "have I been a good colleague to you?" — and then actually practice the _receiving_ rules yourself.
    

---

## ⏳ After the Feedback Is Given

- Give people **time to process** 🕰️ — don't expect instant change.
- You are **not entitled** to a behavior change. It's their choice, and they may adapt differently than you expected.
- Habits die slowly 🐌 — when you notice effort, **acknowledge it**. Don't expect a 180° turn overnight.

---

## ⭐ My Takeaway

Treat others the way I'd want to be treated when receiving feedback myself — lead with **curiosity**, not judgment. This isn't a script to "win" a conversation; it's a relationship-maintenance tool.

**To try next time:**

- [ ] Pause and check emotional state before giving feedback 🌡️
- [ ] Use SBIN instead of vague opinions 📋
- [ ] Balance critique with genuine appreciation ⚖️
- [ ] Ask for feedback back 🔄
